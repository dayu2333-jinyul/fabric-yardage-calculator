How to Calculate Fabric for Bias Binding

Calculating fabric yardage for bias binding involves finding the total area of fabric needed, then working backward to determine the smallest square of fabric that can produce that binding. Here is the formula:

Step 1 — Required Binding Area:
Total binding area = Binding length × Strip width

Step 2 — Fabric Square Size:
Square side = √(Total binding area × (1 + Waste%))
Round up to the nearest inch

Step 3 — Yardage from Square:
Since a square yard = 36" × 36", divide the square side by 36
(Then round up to nearest ⅛ or ¼ yard for purchasing)

For example, you need 168" of 2.25"-wide binding for a baby quilt. Required area: 168 × 2.25 = 378 square inches. An ~19.5" square of fabric gives you ~380 sq in — about ½ yard of 42" wide fabric. Adding 10% waste: round up to ⅝ yard for safety.

Continuous Bias Binding Method

The most efficient way to make bias binding is the continuous method. Cut a square of fabric on the true bias (45°), sew the two cut edges together to form a tube, then cut a continuous spiral of binding. This method produces the longest continuous binding from the smallest piece of fabric — ideal for quilts with curved edges, necklines, or any project that needs flexible, stretchy binding.

Bias Binding Strip Width Guide

The strip width you choose depends on your project and how much fabric you want visible on the finished edge. Standard quilting binding uses 2.25" to 2.5" wide strips, which fold to roughly ⅜" to ½" finished width. Garment bias binding typically uses narrower strips — 1.5" to 2" — for a delicate finish on necklines and armholes. Heavy upholstery binding uses 3" or wider strips for a substantial edge.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between bias binding and straight-grain binding?
Bias binding is cut at a 45-degree angle to the fabric grain, giving it stretch and flexibility to curve smoothly around rounded edges and corners. Straight-grain binding is cut parallel to the selvage — it's more economical but doesn't stretch, so it only works on straight edges. For quilts with curved edges, necklines, or armholes, always use bias binding. The bias binding fabric calculator above estimates for continuous bias strips, which are the most efficient way to make bias binding from a square piece of fabric.
How do I make continuous bias binding from a fabric square?
Cut your fabric square on the diagonal (corner to corner). Sew the two triangles together along the straight-grain edges to form a parallelogram. Mark parallel lines at your strip width along the bias edge, then sew the two short ends together offset by one strip width. Cut along the marked lines in a continuous spiral — you'll get one long strip of bias binding with minimal waste. The calculator above estimates the minimum square size for your required binding length.
How much binding do I need for a quilt?
Measure the quilt perimeter: (length + width) x 2, then add 10" to account for mitered corners and joining the ends. For a queen quilt (90" x 108"), the binding length is (90 + 108) x 2 + 10 = 406 inches. Then decide your strip width — 2.25" is standard. Enter these into the bias binding calculator above to find the exact fabric yardage needed. For our queen example, you need roughly 1 yard of 42" wide fabric.
Can I cut bias binding from a rectangle instead of a square?
Yes. A rectangle cut on the bias produces more total binding length than a square of the same area because the diagonal is longer. The rectangle method is more fabric-efficient but requires more careful layout. For most home sewists, the square method is simpler and the calculator above is designed for square pieces. If you want to use a rectangle, enter your fabric dimensions into our fabric converter and compare the area.
What is the best fabric for bias binding?
Quilting-weight 100% cotton is the most common choice — it presses well, holds a crease, and has enough body to fold neatly. For garments, lightweight cotton, silk, or cotton-linen blends work well. Always prewash your fabric before cutting bias strips to prevent shrinkage after application. Avoid heavy upholstery fabric for bias binding — it's too thick to fold smoothly around corners unless you use a very wide strip (3.5"+) or a single-fold method.
